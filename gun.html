How a Gun Works

Loading:

  • The first step involves loading ammunition into the gun. Ammunition typically consists of a cartridge that includes a bullet, powder, and primer.

Chambering:

  • The loaded cartridge is inserted into the gun's chamber, a specially designed space that holds the cartridge in position.

Closing the Action:

  • The action of the gun is closed, either manually (by operating a slide or bolt) or automatically (by a semi-automatic or automatic mechanism). This action seals the chamber, preparing the gun for firing.

Trigger Mechanism:

  • When the trigger is pulled, it releases a firing pin or striker. In some cases, the trigger pull may cock the gun's internal hammer.

Firing Pin or Striker:

  • The firing pin or striker impacts the primer on the back of the cartridge. The primer contains a small amount of explosive material.

Primer Ignition:

  • The impact of the firing pin ignites the primer, creating a small explosion.

Powder Ignition:

  • The primer's explosion ignites the gunpowder inside the cartridge. This rapid combustion produces a large volume of hot gases.

Bullet Propulsion:

  • The expanding gases push the bullet (projectile) out of the cartridge and through the barrel of the gun.

Barrel:

  • The barrel is a long, metal tube with rifling (spiral grooves) inside. The rifling imparts a spin to the bullet, stabilizing its flight.

Ejection (Semi-Automatic):

  • In semi-automatic firearms, the expended cartridge case is automatically ejected from the gun, making room for the next round to be chambered.

Cycling (Automatic):

  • In automatic firearms, the gun's action cycles automatically, ejecting spent cartridges and chambering new rounds as long as the trigger is held down.

Recoil:

  • The firing of the gun generates recoil, pushing the firearm backward. This is why users feel a backward force when they fire a gun.

Safety Mechanism:

  • Most guns have safety features to prevent accidental discharges. These safety mechanisms can include manual safeties, grip safeties, or trigger safeties.
